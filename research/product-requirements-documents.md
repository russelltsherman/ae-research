# product requirements documents

> Generated by /ae-research → research on 2026-06-25

## Summary
A Product Requirements Document (PRD) is a document that defines a product's purpose, features, functionality, and behavior, serving as a single source of truth that aligns cross-functional teams (product managers, designers, developers, testers, and stakeholders) on what to build and why. Its defining characteristic is a focus on "what" the product should do from the user's perspective, while deliberately avoiding "how" it will be built — implementation detail is deferred to a downstream Functional Specification (Technical Requirements Document). There is no single universal PRD structure; templates range from lean one-pagers to comprehensive multi-section documents and vary by author and purpose, but a recurring core set of sections emerges across sources: overview/purpose, goals and success metrics, target users/personas and user stories, functional and non-functional requirements (often with acceptance criteria), design/UX, assumptions/constraints/dependencies, out-of-scope items, and release/rollout considerations. AI tooling such as ChatPRD now generates PRDs and related artifacts from prompts or rough notes, marketing dramatic time savings ("minutes, not days"), though those efficiency figures are vendor claims rather than independently verified benchmarks.

## Caveats
Source quality is skewed toward vendor/blog material (Atlassian, Productboard, ProductPlan, Miro, Product School, AltexSoft, monday.com, Planio, ChatPRD) plus Wikipedia as the lone encyclopedic secondary source. This is acceptable here because the central claims are definitional/conceptual consensus about a stable, slow-moving artifact rather than extraordinary empirical claims — many definitions were confirmed verbatim and cross-corroborated by 5+ independent sources. Two specific weaknesses: (1) PRD section lists are prescriptive conventions, not standards — sources explicitly disagree on exact section sets, and two over-specified fixed-template claims were refuted 0-3, so any single section list should be treated as one author's recommendation, not a canonical schema. (2) The ChatPRD time-savings claim ('minutes, not days') is vendor marketing copy, not an independently benchmarked result; treat the magnitude of savings as unverified. There is also a known critical counter-current in the field (Reforge's 'Is the PRD Dead?', 'Your PRD Is a Waste of Time') arguing that static PRDs go stale and fail in practice — these critique HOW PRDs are used, not the definition, but they qualify the 'single source of truth' framing in real-world use. Time-sensitivity is low for definitions/structure; higher for the AI-tooling landscape (ChatPRD figures are June 2026 and will drift).

## Open questions
- How effective are PRDs in practice versus in principle? Critics (Reforge, et al.) argue static PRDs go stale; the evidence here establishes intended role but not measured outcome effectiveness.
- Are the ChatPRD time-savings claims ('minutes, not days') supported by any independent, controlled benchmark, or only by vendor copy and user anecdotes?
- How do PRDs interact with agile/lean practices — when does a team use a full PRD versus a one-pager, user-story backlog, or no PRD at all? Sources mention the range but not decision criteria.
- What is the precise downstream relationship between a PRD and a Functional/Technical Requirements Document in modern (vs. traditional waterfall) workflows, given that the PRD-to-FRD decomposition is described mainly by Wikipedia?
