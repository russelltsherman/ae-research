# intent engineering

> Generated by /ae-research → research on 2026-06-25

## Summary
"Intent engineering" denotes two related-but-distinct things in the verified sources. In the dominant, emerging (2025-2026) AI-agent usage, intent engineering is the structured design of AI systems around goals, constraints, and measurable outcomes — defining *what must be accomplished* and how success is judged, as a persistent system-level specification that governs agent behavior when explicit prompts run out, in contrast to prompt engineering (how a request is phrased) and context engineering (what information the model can access). It is operationalized concretely (e.g., SQUER's eleven-field "Intent" document and a dedicated "Intent Engineer" role) and is closely tied to Spec-Driven Development, where an explicit spec is the durable "connective tissue" linking business intent to architecture, code, tests, and validation. A separate, older lineage uses "intent" technically: RFC 9315 defines intent for networking as declarative operational goals/outcomes without specifying implementation (distinct from event-condition-action policy), and a peer-reviewed pipeline treats intent recognition for conversational AI as gathering, normalizing, and coverage-ordering intents from a corpus. Caveat: nearly all of the "intent engineering as discipline" sources are vendor/thought-leadership blogs from a single recent window, so the conceptual core is well-corroborated but not academically settled.

## Findings

Tericsoft: 'the structured design of AI systems around goals, constraints, and measurable outcomes rather than surface-level instructions.' MindStudio: 'the practice of defining the goal with enough clarity and structure that an AI system can pursue it reliably.' Tericsoft on instruction-vs-intent: 'An instruction tells the system what to generate. Intent tells the system what must be accomplished.' All sources are vendor/thought-leadership blogs (medium quality) but cross-corroborate; claims 4 and 13 carried one dissenting vote each, claim 6 was unanimous.
  - Sources: https://www.tericsoft.com/blogs/intent-engineering, https://www.mindstudio.ai/blog/prompt-engineering-vs-context-engineering-vs-intent-engineering, https://www.productcompass.pm/p/intent-engineering-framework-for-ai-agents
- **Intent is distinguished from a prompt: a prompt is a momentary, one-off, conversation-level instruction, whereas intent is a persistent, product/system-level specification that governs agent behavior when explicit instructions run out or are ambiguous.** — confidence: high
  - Evidence: Product Compass: 'A prompt is a momentary instruction for a specific request. Intent is a system-level specification that persists across interactions and governs behavior when prompts are incomplete or ambiguous'; 'Intent is what determines how an agent acts when instructions run out'; 'Intent engineering is not prompt writing. It is system design.' MindStudio: 'prompt engineering asks how you phrase a request, intent engineering asks what you're trying to achieve.' Pathmode: prompt engineering is 'the craft of writing a single instruction... tactical and conversational,' while intent engineering 'structures the entire problem upfront.' All five constituent claims passed 3-0; multiple independent sources agree.
  - Sources: https://www.productcompass.pm/p/intent-engineering-framework-for-ai-agents, https://www.mindstudio.ai/blog/prompt-engineering-vs-context-engineering-vs-intent-engineering, https://pathmode.io/glossary/intent-engineering, https://www.tericsoft.com/blogs/intent-engineering
- **Intent engineering is distinguished from context engineering and sits at a higher level of abstraction: context engineering optimizes what information a model can access, while intent engineering specifies the goal and success criteria upstream of context.** — confidence: high
  - Evidence: Tericsoft: 'Context Engineering optimizes what information is available. Intent Engineering optimizes what must be achieved.' MindStudio: 'Context engineering manages what information an AI model has access to, while intent engineering specifies the goal and the criteria for success. Both constituent claims passed 3-0; corroborated by Pathmode (context = inputs, intent = problem definition).
  - Sources: https://www.tericsoft.com/blogs/intent-engineering, https://www.mindstudio.ai/blog/prompt-engineering-vs-context-engineering-vs-intent-engineering, https://pathmode.io/glossary/intent-engineering

  - Evidence: Microsoft Developer blog: 'Spec-Driven Development (SDD) is a spec-first approach. - **In intent-based networking, RFC 9315 defines intent as a set of declarative operational goals and desired outcomes for a network without specifying how to implement them, explicitly distinct from policy, which uses a user-articulated event-condition-action rule model.** — confidence: high
  - Evidence: RFC 9315 (IRTF, Oct 2022), Section 2: intent is 'A set of operational goals (that a network should meet) and outcomes (that a network is supposed to deliver) defined in a declarative manner without specifying how to achieve or implement them.' ## Caveats
Source quality is the central caveat. The dominant "intent engineering as an AI discipline" findings (Findings 1-5) rest almost entirely on vendor and thought-leadership blogs (Tericsoft, MindStudio, Product Compass, Pathmode, SQUER, Microsoft Developer) published in a single narrow window (2025-2026). The term is emerging and marketing-adjacent, with no peer-reviewed or standards-body backing; the conceptual core is well-corroborated across independent blogs, but definitions, field boundaries, and the prompt/context/intent hierarchy are not academically settled and could shift. Several authors are promoting tooling or coined roles (SQUER coined "Intent Engineer"; MindStudio/Tericsoft sell agent tooling), so promotional bias is present even though the specific definitional claims are echoed by non-vendor sources. Time-sensitivity is high for Findings 1-5 — expect terminology to consolidate or be relabeled. By contrast, Findings 6 (RFC 9315) and 7 (Springer pipeline) are primary, durable, peer-reviewed/standards sources, but they use "intent" in older, narrower technical senses (networking; conversational-AI intent classification) that are conceptually distinct from the AI-agent "intent engineering" discipline — readers should not conflate the lineages. Springer and ResearchGate copies were paywalled/403 during verification (text confirmed via institutional abstract and independent search), and the Tericsoft exact-quote wording could not be byte-for-byte confirmed via WebFetch for one claim (Finding 3) though the substance was multiply corroborated. The SQUER "eleven fields" source internally contradicts itself (says eleven, lists nine). Five related claims were refuted (e.g., Intent Engineer as strictly business-side; broad router-agent definition; intent-files-vs-passive-specs framings) and should not be repeated as fact.

## Open questions
- Will 'intent engineering' consolidate into a stable, distinct discipline, or be absorbed into 'context engineering' / 'spec-driven development' as the terminology matures? The boundaries currently overlap heavily across sources.
- Is there any peer-reviewed or standards-body treatment of the AI-agent sense of 'intent engineering' (as opposed to the networking RFC 9315 sense), or does all current authority come from vendor/practitioner blogs?
- How, concretely and reproducibly, is 'intent' validated against outcomes in practice (the Intent Engineer's third function) — what metrics, tooling, or evaluation loops are actually used, beyond the conceptual claim?
- What is the relationship (if any) between the declarative intent concept in intent-based networking (RFC 9315) and the AI-agent intent-engineering discipline — convergent terminology by coincidence, or shared declarative-specification roots?
