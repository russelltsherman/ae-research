# particle accelerator

> Generated by /ae-research → research on 2026-06-26

## Summary
A particle accelerator drives charged particles to high energies using electromagnetic fields, then collides or directs them for research, medical, and industrial use. The dominant accelerator architectures are linear accelerators (linacs), cyclotrons (fixed magnets + alternating fields, spiral path), and synchrotrons (ramped magnetic field at constant radius). The flagship research machine is CERN's Large Hadron Collider, a ~27 km circular collider at the Franco-Swiss border that reaches a record 13.6 TeV proton-proton collision energy and broke luminosity records in 2025. Performance frontiers are set by magnet and RF technology: superconducting Nb-Ti (~8-9 T) limits the LHC, while Nb3Sn and HTS magnets (>12-17 T) and superconducting RF cavities (Q0 of 10^9-10^10, operating at 1.9 K) underpin upgrades like HL-LHC and proposed machines (FCC, HE-LHC). Most of the world's ~50,000 accelerators serve industry and medicine, not pure research; radiotherapy linacs and proton-therapy systems are the largest applied categories. Emerging plasma acceleration (~100 GV/m) points toward far more compact future machines. Findings below carry per-claim sources and confidence; nearly all rest on unanimous votes against primary sources.

## Findings
- **Particle accelerators come in three principal architectures: cyclotrons use alternating electric fields and fixed magnets to drive charged particles along a circular spiral path; synchrotrons are circular accelerators using alternating voltage combined with an increasing magnetic field strength (to hold a constant-radius orbit); both contrast with linear accelerators.** — confidence: high
  - Evidence: Merges claims [1] and [2]. OpenStax/LibreTexts states near-verbatim that a cyclotron 'uses alternating electric fields and fixed magnets to accelerate particles in a circular spiral path' and a synchrotron 'is a circular accelerator that uses alternating voltage and increasing magnetic field strength.' Corroborated by Britannica: synchrotron confines particles to a constant circular orbit while increasing field strength to keep orbit radius constant. Settled physics (cyclotron 1930s, synchrotron 1940s). Both 3-0 votes. Minor nuance: 'fixed magnets' means a static magnetic field, and 'alternating voltage' refers to RF cavities.
  - Sources: https://phys.libretexts.org/Bookshelves/University_Physics/University_Physics_(OpenStax)/University_Physics_III_-_Optics_and_Modern_Physics_(OpenStax)/11:_Particle_Physics_and_Cosmology/11.05:_Particle_Accelerators_and_Detectors, https://www.britannica.com/technology/particle-accelerator/Synchrotrons
- **The Large Hadron Collider is the world's largest particle accelerator: a circular machine ~27 km (17 mi) in circumference, housed in a tunnel ~100 m underground at the Franco-Swiss border near Geneva.** — confidence: high
  - Evidence: Merges the geometry/scale portions of claims [0] and [3]. CERN's primary page gives circumference 26,659 m (~27 km) and tunnel '100 metres underground' at the Franco-Swiss border near Geneva; OpenStax states '17 miles in circumference' and 'currently the largest accelerator in the world.' Wikipedia confirms 'world's largest and highest-energy particle accelerator.' Both 3-0. Minor nuance: actual tunnel depth ranges 50-175 m, so 100 m is a representative figure CERN itself cites.
  - Sources: https://home.cern/science/accelerators/large-hadron-collider/, https://phys.libretexts.org/Bookshelves/University_Physics/University_Physics_(OpenStax)/University_Physics_III_-_Optics_and_Modern_Physics_(OpenStax)/11:_Particle_Physics_and_Cosmology/11.05:_Particle_Accelerators_and_Detectors, https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large_Hadron_Collider
- **The LHC collides protons at a center-of-mass energy of 13.6 TeV (beam energy 6.8 TeV each in Run 3) - the highest energy ever achieved in a laboratory - enabling creation of particles whose total mass corresponds to that available energy.** — confidence: high
  - Evidence: Merges the energy portions of claims [0] and [4]. CERN states verbatim '13.6 TeV, which is the highest energy ever reached in a Lab.' The conservative OpenStax framing (beam energy >6 TeV, total >12 TeV) holds under both Run 2 (13 TeV) and Run 3 (13.6 TeV). ATLAS/CMS corroborate Run 3 collisions at sqrt(s)=13.6 TeV (beam 6.8 TeV each) since July 2022. The 'in a laboratory' qualifier is correct (cosmic rays reach higher but are not lab-produced). Both 3-0. Currency: record stands as of June 2026; 14 TeV and HL-LHC are planned, not yet realized.
  - Sources: https://home.cern/science/accelerators/large-hadron-collider/, https://phys.libretexts.org/Bookshelves/University_Physics/University_Physics_(OpenStax)/University_Physics_III_-_Optics_and_Modern_Physics_(OpenStax)/11:_Particle_Physics_and_Cosmology/11.05:_Particle_Accelerators_and_Detectors, https://atlas.cern/
- **The LHC set integrated-luminosity records: in 2025 it delivered 125 fb^-1 to each of ATLAS and CMS, bringing each experiment's lifetime delivered luminosity to 500 fb^-1, equal to roughly 50 million billion particle collisions.** — confidence: high
  - Evidence: Merges claims [5] and [6]. CERN news (9 Dec 2025) states verbatim 'delivering 125 fb^-1 to both the ATLAS and the CMS experiments' (a new integrated-luminosity record) and 'ATLAS and CMS have now each been delivered an integrated luminosity of 500 fb^-1, equating to approximately 50 million billion particle collisions.' Corroborated by phys.org. Both 3-0. Single but authoritative primary source (the LHC operator); current as of Dec 2025.
  - Sources: https://home.cern/news/news/accelerators/lhc-delivers-record-number-particle-collisions-2025
- **The proposed Future Circular Collider (FCC) would be a 90.7 km underground ring beneath Haute-Savoie and Ain (France) and the Swiss canton of Geneva; its first stage (FCC-ee) would collide electrons with positrons as a 'Higgs factory' for high-precision Higgs-boson study.** — confidence: high
  - Evidence: Merges claims [7] and [8]. CERN's FCC page gives circumference '90.7 kilometres' beneath Haute-Savoie, Ain, and canton of Geneva (avg depth ~200 m), and states the first stage 'would collide electrons with their antiparticles' as a 'Higgs factory.' An electron's antiparticle is the positron. The 2025 FCC Feasibility Study (arXiv:2505.00274), CERN Courier, and Wikipedia corroborate FCC-ee as the first-stage e+e- factory (~3 million Higgs bosons over ~15 yrs, first collisions early 2040s). Both 3-0. Proposal/planning stage, current to 2024-2026 layout.
  - Sources: https://home.cern/science/accelerators/future-circular-collider/, https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.00274
- **Reaching higher collider energies is gated by magnet field strength. Nb-Ti superconductor (used in the LHC's 1232 dipoles at ~8.3 T) maxes out at ~8-9 T; the FCC-hh would need thousands of Nb3Sn magnets at 16-17 T, and HE-LHC/FCC upgrades require Nb3Sn and high-temperature superconductors (HTS) capable of fields exceeding 12 T.** — confidence: high
  - Evidence: Merges claims [9] and [20]. The Frontiers paper states Nb-Ti's max reachable field is '8-9 T, as for the LHC dipole magnets' and the FCC-hh needs 'a few 1,000 of Nb3Sn magnets with a higher field of 16 or 17 T.' IntechOpen states HE-LHC/FCC need 'Nb3Sn and high-temperature superconductors (HTS), capable of achieving fields exceeding 12 T.' CERN/Fermilab confirm fields above 10-12 T became possible with Nb3Sn. Both 3-0. Caveat: CERN Courier notes the FCC-hh baseline was later rebaselined from 16 T toward ~14 T for viability - still above 12 T and still requiring Nb3Sn.
  - Sources: https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/physics/articles/10.3389/fphy.2022.888395/full, https://www.intechopen.com/online-first/1217412, https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large_Hadron_Collider
- **The High-Luminosity LHC (HL-LHC) targets a peak luminosity of 5 x 10^34 cm^-2 s^-1 per interaction point, five times the original LHC design value.** — confidence: high
  - Evidence: Claim [10]. IntechOpen states the HL-LHC targets '5 x 10^34 per interaction point, which is five times the LHC's original design value.' Original LHC design peak luminosity is 1 x 10^34 cm^-2 s^-1; CERN HiLumi and Wikipedia confirm the 5x (to 5-7x) increase. The 7.5 x 10^34 'ultimate/levelled' figure in some sources is a stretch value, not a contradiction. 3-0. Minor: source omitted units.
  - Sources: https://www.intechopen.com/online-first/1217412, https://arxiv.org/abs/1705.08830
- **Superconducting RF (SRF) cavities reach intrinsic quality factors (Q0) of 10^9-10^10, far above copper, because a superconductor's surface resistance is many orders of magnitude lower; this yields a net operating-power gain of several hundred over copper even after liquid-helium refrigeration overhead.** — confidence: high
  - Evidence: Merges claims [11] and [13]. Padamsee (arXiv:1501.07129, foremost SRF authority) states verbatim that SRF surface resistance is 'many orders of magnitude less than that of copper' giving Q0 'in the 10^9 to 10^10 range,' and that 'after accounting for the refrigerator power... a net gain factor of several hundred remains.' Corroborated by CERN reviews and the TESLA-cavity paper (Nb ~nano-ohm vs copper milli-ohm surface resistance). Wikipedia notes SRF cuts cooling-driven operating cost by ~1000x. Claim [11] 3-0; claim [13] 2-1 (the verbatim quote was confirmed in the primary PDF). The 10^9-10^10 range is conservative; modern N-doped cavities exceed it.
  - Sources: https://arxiv.org/pdf/1501.07129, https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superconducting_radio_frequency
- **Copper (normal-conducting) cavities are limited to accelerating gradients near 1 MV/m in CW and long-pulse (duty factor >1%) operation because RF power capital/operating costs become prohibitive - e.g. several MW/m of RF power are needed to run a copper cavity at just 5 MV/m.** — confidence: high
  - Evidence: Claim [12]. Padamsee (arXiv:1501.07129) states verbatim copper cavities 'are limited to gradients near 1 MV/m in CW and long-pulse (duty factor > 1%) operation because the capital cost of the RF power and the ac-power related operating cost become prohibitive... several MW/m of RF power are required to operate a copper cavity at 5 MV/m.' Podlech (arXiv:1303.6552) confirms the same thermal/ohmic-loss physics. 3-0. Correctly scoped to CW/long-pulse; copper reaches far higher gradients in short-pulse/low-duty use (CLIC, X-band).
  - Sources: https://arxiv.org/pdf/1501.07129, https://arxiv.org/abs/1303.6552
- **LHC interaction-region magnets currently use NbTi quadrupoles with a 70 mm aperture and a 200 T/m field gradient; superconducting accelerator magnets such as the HL-LHC Nb3Sn quadrupole (MQXF) and FRESCA2-class dipoles are designed to operate at 1.9 K (superfluid helium).** — confidence: high
  - Evidence: Merges claims [14] and [15]. Ferracin et al. (arXiv:1501.07173, CERN) state 'the LHC interaction regions feature Nb-Ti quadrupole magnets with a 70 mm aperture and a gradient of 200 T/m' and pose an exercise to 'design a Nb3Sn superconducting quadrupole magnet with an aperture of 150 mm, operating at 1.9 K' (MQXF for the HL-LHC IR upgrade), with FRESCA2 a large-aperture Nb3Sn dipole at 1.9 K. CERN HiLumi confirms HL-LHC Nb3Sn quads run in superfluid He at 1.9 K. Both 3-0. Currency: original NbTi triplets still installed as of June 2026; Nb3Sn replacements scheduled for LS3.
  - Sources: https://arxiv.org/pdf/1501.07173
- **Most particle accelerators are not for fundamental research: more than half of all accelerators are used for industrial applications and most of the remainder for medical applications.** — confidence: high
  - Evidence: Claim [16]. arXiv:2407.10216v1 states 'Over half of those accelerators are used for industrial applications, and most of the rest are used for medical applications.' Widely-cited 2014 figures (~27,000 industry / ~14,000 medical / ~1,200 research of ~42,200 total) match the qualitative ordering. 3-0. Note: the related claim of 'over 50,000 accelerators worldwide' was refuted (1-2) - sources differ on the exact total, but not on the industry-dominant/medical-second distribution.
  - Sources: https://arxiv.org/html/2407.10216v1
- **Medical accelerators dominate applied use: ~12,500 radiotherapy linacs operate worldwide (typically 6-25 MeV electron beams), and proton-therapy clinical requirements call for proton extraction energies of 60-240 MeV.** — confidence: high
  - Evidence: Merges claims [17] and [18]. arXiv:2407.10216v1 states '~12,500 radiotherapy linacs in the world' with beams 'typically between 6 and 25 MeV.' PMC3473892 Table 1 lists proton extraction energy min/max as '60, 240 MeV.' IAEA (Sept 2025) corroborates 'more than 12,000 linacs in operation.' Both 3-0. Vendor figures vary slightly (230 vs 240 MeV, 70 vs 60 MeV min) but all fall within this clinical envelope.
  - Sources: https://arxiv.org/html/2407.10216v1, https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3473892/
- **Plasma acceleration routinely achieves accelerating fields of ~100 GV/m - roughly 3,000 times the superconducting Nb RF cavities proposed for the International Linear Collider - pointing toward far more compact future accelerators.** — confidence: high
  - Evidence: Claim [19]. The Frontiers paper (Zimmermann, CERN) states 'plasma acceleration routinely achieves fields of 100 GV/m, which is 3,000 times higher than the Nb cavities proposed for the International Linear Collider' (100 GV/m / 31.5 MV/m ILC spec ~= 3,175x). Nature Communications 2021 demonstrated a mm-scale plasma accelerator >100 GV/m (128 MeV gain). 3-0. Caveat: 'routinely' overstates maturity - peak gradients are demonstrated, but sustained collider-grade, beam-quality operation remains a research challenge.
  - Sources: https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/physics/articles/10.3389/fphy.2022.888395/full, https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-021-23000-7
- **Synchrotron-radiation/heat power is a fundamental design constraint for future circular colliders: FCC-ee adopts a 100 MW total synchrotron-radiation power budget, and FCC-hh heat removal requires more than 100 MW of cryoplant power.** — confidence: high
  - Evidence: Claim [21]. The Frontiers paper states 'for the proposed future circular electron-positron collider FCC-ee a total constant value of 100 MW has been adopted as a design constraint' and that FCC-hh heat removal 'requires more than 100 MW of electrical cryoplant power'; for both, 'synchrotron radiation alone implies more than 100 MW of electrical power needs.' Corroborated by the 2025 FCC Feasibility Study (arXiv:2505.00274), which retains the FCC-ee 100 MW SR budget (50 MW/beam). 3-0. Minor nuance: the FCC-ee 100 MW is specifically the synchrotron-radiation power budget, not generic total facility power.
  - Sources: https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/physics/articles/10.3389/fphy.2022.888395/full, https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.00274

## Caveats
Time sensitivity: LHC operational records are current as of Dec 2025/June 2026 - the 13.6 TeV record, 125 fb^-1 (2025) and 500 fb^-1 (lifetime) figures will be superseded as Run 3 continues and HL-LHC begins (~2030). FCC and HE-LHC are proposals, not approved/built machines; specs (e.g. FCC-hh magnet field rebaselined from 16-17 T toward ~14 T) are evolving design targets, not realized hardware. Source strength: most claims rest on primary sources (CERN, arXiv technical papers, peer-reviewed journals) with unanimous 3-0 votes; the SRF net-power-gain claim [13] passed only 2-1 though its verbatim quote was confirmed in the primary PDF. Several LHC operational facts (2025/lifetime luminosity) rely on a single CERN news release - authoritative as the operator, but single-sourced. Three claims from the original pool were refuted and excluded: the 11 T Nb3Sn collimation-dipole detail (0-3), the '50,000 accelerators worldwide' total (1-2; sources disagree on the count though not the distribution), and laser-driven proton energies of 70-100 MeV (0-3). Textbook framings (e.g. 'particles with total mass exceeding 12 TeV', 'fixed magnets') are simplifications faithful to their cited source but slightly loose physically.

## Open questions
- What is the actual current total number of particle accelerators in operation worldwide? The commonly cited '50,000' figure was refuted (vote 1-2) and 2014 surveys put the total near 42,000 - the count is unsettled.
- Will the FCC project be approved, and at what final magnet field (16-17 T vs the rebaselined ~14 T) and energy will FCC-hh be built, if at all?
- How close is plasma/laser-driven acceleration to producing collider-grade, beam-quality, sustained operation rather than demonstrated peak gradients - i.e. when does the '100 GV/m, more compact accelerator' promise become a usable machine?
- What are the current state-of-the-art laser-driven proton energies, given the 70-100 MeV claim was refuted (0-3)?
