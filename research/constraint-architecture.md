# constraint architecture

> Generated by /ae-research → research on 2026-06-25

## Summary
"Constraint architecture" spans several distinct domains, each with its own well-established architectural pattern. In constraint programming (CP) systems, two complementary architectures dominate: a microkernel design (Michel & Van Hentenryck) that reduces the core to event scheduling/propagation while keeping variables, domains, and constraints external as layered services, and the procedural/constraint-store model (Bockmayr & Hooker) where each constraint carries its own filtering algorithm and a shared constraint store propagates domain restrictions between them. In geometric constraint solving for parametric CAD, the dominant architecture is a two-phase graph-constructive pipeline — Phase 1 builds and decomposes a weighted constraint graph (vertices = objects' DOF, edges = DOF removed by constraints) into a solution plan, Phase 2 recursively solves it — with concrete instances like the S-DR decomposition-recombination algorithm built on skeletons. A hardware variant exists: an event-based architecture of coupled analog oscillators and asynchronous digital circuits that solves CSPs/SAT via non-repeating oscillator phase relations, scaling comparably to probSAT. In software engineering governance, "constraint architecture" maps onto architectural fitness functions (Ford/Parsons/Kua) — objective, automated guardrails that constrain architectural characteristics — and tools like ArchUnit that enforce such constraints as bytecode-analyzing unit-test rules in a fluent DSL. All findings rest on primary or originator sources with unanimous 3-0 verification votes.

## Findings
- **In constraint programming, a microkernel architecture reduces the core to event scheduling and propagation, keeping variables, domains, and constraints external as layered services.** — confidence: high
  - Evidence: Michel & Van Hentenryck's 'A Microkernel Architecture for Constraint Programming' (Constraints 22(2), 2017; arXiv preprint 1401.5334) presents a microkernel organized around 'a number of small number of core functionalities and minimal interfaces' contrasting with monolithic implementations. The abstract states 'variables, domains, and constraints all remain completely external to the microkernel... the microkernel isolates the propagation logic and events protocols from the variables and constraint definitions.' The Objective-CP kernel exposes a minimalistic API with two capabilities — scheduling events and propagating events — and 'Each computational domain (e.g., booleans, reals, integers, sets) becomes a service on top of the microkernel.' All three sub-claims verified verbatim against the primary source.
  - Sources: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10601-016-9242-1, https://arxiv.org/abs/1401.5334
- **An alternative CP architecture treats each constraint as a procedure with its own filtering algorithm, linked through a shared constraint store; global constraints encapsulate structured constraint sets and invoke specialized technology.** — confidence: high
  - Evidence: Bockmayr & Hooker's 'Constraint Programming' handbook chapter (2003) titles Section 1.1 'Constraints as Procedures': each constraint is viewed as a procedure operating on the solution space, contrasting with 'the traditional approach used in optimization, in which the solver receives the problem as a set of undifferentiated constraints.' The constraint store links these procedures: 'Each procedure applies a filtering algorithm that eliminates some values from the variable domains... The restricted domains are in effect in-domain constraints... They become part of the constraint store, which is passed on to the next constraint to be processed.' A global constraint 'is a single constraint that represents a highly structured set of constraints' (e.g. alldifferent over pairwise disequations) and 'can be designed to invoke the best known technology for dealing with its particular structure' (corroborated by Régin's GAC matching algorithm for alldifferent). All quotes extracted verbatim from the primary PDF.
  - Sources: https://johnhooker.tepper.cmu.edu/cp-hb.pdf
- **The dominant architecture for geometric constraint solving in parametric CAD is a two-phase graph-constructive pipeline: graph analysis/decomposition produces a solution plan, then a solver recursively elaborates it.** — confidence: high
  - Evidence: Bettig & Hoffmann, 'Geometric Constraint Solving in Parametric CAD' (ASME J. Computing and Information Science in Engineering 11(2):021001, 2011): 'the dominant approach to constraint solving is based on a constraint graph analysis that formulates a solution plan, followed by a solver that, usually recursively, elaborates the plan and computes a solution.' 'Graph-constructive solvers have become the dominant class... builds first a graph representing the constraint problem for the purpose of isolating specific, small subsets... In a second phase, the solvers then recursively solve the actual constraints, guided by the graph decomposition.' The four overall approaches are 'graph-based, logic-based, algebraic, and theorem prover-based.' Graph-constructive subdivides into top-down (Owen 1991), bottom-up (Bouma et al. 1993), and hybrid, both pioneering methods being O(n^2) in Phase 1. Independently corroborated by Fudos & Hoffmann 1997 and the 2022 arXiv review 2202.13795. Caveat: 2011 source predates recent ML/neural approaches.
  - Sources: https://www.cs.purdue.edu/cgvlab/www/resources/papers/Bettig-Comp_and_Info_Sci_in_Eng-2011-Geometric_Constraint_Solving_In_Parametric_CAD.pdf, https://asmedigitalcollection.asme.org/computingengineering/article/11/2/021001/465813/Geometric-Constraint-Solving-in-Parametric
- **In the graph-based geometric constraint representation, vertices are geometric objects weighted by their degrees of freedom (2D point=2, circle=3) and edges are constraints weighted by the DOF they remove (incidence=2, distance=1).** — confidence: high
  - Evidence: Bettig & Hoffmann (ASME 2011): 'The graph vertices are labeled with the number of degrees of freedom of the represented geometric object. In 2D, a point would have 2 degrees of freedom, a circle 3. Each graph edge is labeled by the degrees of freedom... an incidence constraint cancels 2, a distance constraint cancels 1 degree of freedom.' Also: 'The graph that is analyzed in Phase 1 has vertices representing the geometric objects to be instantiated and edges that represent constraints between them. Both vertices v and edges e are labeled with positive integral weights. The weight w(v)... represents the degrees of freedom when placing the corresponding geometric object.' This is the standard weighted/Laman-graph formulation, corroborated by arXiv review 2202.13795. Minor nuance: the specific ASME quote defines vertex weight explicitly; edge-weight semantics rest on the universal DOC convention.
  - Sources: https://www.cs.purdue.edu/cgvlab/www/resources/papers/Bettig-Comp_and_Info_Sci_in_Eng-2011-Geometric_Constraint_Solving_In_Parametric_CAD.pdf, https://asmedigitalcollection.asme.org/computingengineering/article/11/2/021001/465813/Geometric-Constraint-Solving-in-Parametric
- **A concrete instance of the graph-constructive architecture is the S-DR decomposition-recombination planner, built on a graph reduction method and organized around 'skeletons', which decomposes a well-constrained 2D system into sub-systems and recombines their solutions.** — confidence: high
  - Evidence: Springer chapter 10.1007/3-540-47997-X_13 (and journal extension S0965997810001006): 'a decomposition-recombination (DR) planning algorithm, called S-DR, that uses a graph reduction method to solve systems of 2D geometric constraints... the S-DR planner figures out a plan for decomposing a well constrained system into small sub-systems and recombines the solutions of these sub-systems to derive the solution of the entire system.' The method is explicitly 'Based on the key concept of skeletons.' The Springer chapter itself could not be fetched directly (auth redirect), but specifics are independently verified via the ScienceDirect journal extension, NYU Scholars, and ResearchGate.
  - Sources: https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/3-540-47997-X_13, https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0965997810001006
- **A hardware/physical constraint architecture solves CSPs and SAT using a network of nodes, each combining an analog oscillator and an asynchronous digital circuit communicating via digital pulses, driven by non-repeating phase relations among incommensurable oscillators rather than external noise; on random SAT it scales comparably to probSAT.** — confidence: high
  - Evidence: Mostafa, Mueller & Indiveri, 'An event-based architecture for solving constraint satisfaction problems' (Nature Communications, 2015; PMC4686837): 'Each node has...an analogue oscillator and a state-holding asynchronous digital circuit'; 'The nodes communicate using digital pulses, or events'; nodes represent problem variables (plus helper variables). The architecture 'relies on the non-repeating phase relations among these incommensurable analogue oscillators to drive the search for optimal solutions, rather than making use of external sources of noise or relying on random fluctuations.' On SAT: 'it is therefore reasonable to assume that for large problems the network performs as well as the standard algorithm [probSAT] in terms of number of flips to solution.' Caveat: equivalence is measured in flips-to-solution, not wall-clock; authors note they cannot compare against state-of-the-art benchmarks due to simulator speed.
  - Sources: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC4686837/
- **In software-architecture governance, 'constraint architecture' is realized through architectural fitness functions: objective, measurable, automated guardrails that assess and constrain architectural characteristics as a system evolves.** — confidence: high
  - Evidence: Ford, Parsons & Kua, 'Building Evolutionary Architectures' (O'Reilly, ch.2) define: 'An architectural fitness function provides an objective integrity assessment of some architectural characteristic(s)' and 'The fitness function protects the various architectural characteristics required for the system.' InfoQ gives the canonical full form: 'An architectural fitness function is any mechanism that provides an objective integrity assessment of some architectural characteristic(s)' and frames them as 'self-defined guardrails for certain aspects of our architecture.' The 2nd edition (2023) is subtitled 'Automated Software Governance' with a chapter 'Automating Architectural Governance.' Corroborated by ThoughtWorks Technology Radar and continuous-architecture.org. Note: these are originator/secondary sources rather than independent primary research, but they are the canonical defining sources for the concept.
  - Sources: https://nealford.com/books/buildingevolutionaryarchitectures.html, https://www.oreilly.com/library/view/building-evolutionary-architectures/9781491986356/ch02.html, https://www.infoq.com/articles/fitness-functions-architecture/
- **ArchUnit is a concrete tool that enforces architectural constraints as code: it analyzes compiled Java bytecode, imports classes into an internal structure, and checks declarative fluent-DSL rules ('classes that <predicate> should <condition>') as ordinary unit tests.** — confidence: high
  - Evidence: ArchUnit User Guide (primary): 'ArchUnit is a free, simple and extensible library for checking the architecture of your Java code'; 'ArchUnit's main focus is to automatically test architecture and coding rules, using any plain Java unit testing framework'; 'It does so by analyzing given Java bytecode, importing all classes into a Java code structure.' Rules use a fluent DSL, e.g. verbatim from the guide: classes().that().resideInAPackage("..service..").should().onlyBeAccessed().byAnyPackage("..controller..", "..service.."). Corroborated by the TNG/ArchUnit GitHub repo, ArchUnit-Examples, and Baeldung. This is the operational counterpart to fitness-function governance — constraints expressed and enforced as automated tests.
  - Sources: https://www.archunit.org/userguide/html/000_Index.html, https://github.com/TNG/ArchUnit

## Caveats
The term "constraint architecture" is not a single concept — the verified claims cluster into at least four largely unrelated domains (CP solver internals, geometric/CAD constraint solving, neuromorphic hardware CSP solvers, and software-architecture governance), so any synthesis is a survey across fields rather than a unified answer. Source-strength varies: the CP, CAD, and hardware findings rest on peer-reviewed primary literature with verbatim verification, while the fitness-function findings rely largely on the concept's originators (Ford/Parsons/Kua) and reputable secondary outlets (InfoQ, ThoughtWorks) rather than independent empirical research — these are definitional/conceptual claims, not measured results. Time-sensitivity: the CAD architecture source is from 2011 and the 2022 arXiv review notes a research shift toward general constraint systems and ML/neural approaches that this synthesis does not cover; the CP handbook chapter is from 2003 (though it describes stable foundational concepts). Two specific quote-support gaps were flagged by verifiers but judged non-fatal: the edge-weight semantics in the graph-based representation rest on universal convention rather than the exact cited ASME quote, and the Springer S-DR chapter could not be fetched directly (verified instead via its journal extension and independent listings). The Nature Communications hardware result measures SAT-solving equivalence in flips-to-solution, not wall-clock time, and the authors explicitly disclaim comparison against state-of-the-art solvers.
