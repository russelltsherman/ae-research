# prompt engineering

> Generated by /ae-research → research on 2026-06-25

## Summary
Prompt engineering is the practice of steering an LLM's output through carefully constructed input text rather than retraining or fine-tuning its parameters, operating solely on the model's embedded knowledge. The field has matured into systematically catalogued surveys (The Prompt Report's 58 text + 40 multimodal techniques; Sahoo et al.'s 41 techniques across 12 domains; Vatsal & Dubey's 39 methods from 44 papers) and a set of empirically validated techniques. The highest-leverage techniques with documented benchmark gains are reasoning scaffolds — chain-of-thought (an emergent ability of large models, improving arithmetic/commonsense/symbolic reasoning), self-consistency (e.g. +17.9% on GSM8K over CoT), and tree-of-thoughts (74% vs 4% on Game of 24) — alongside structural practices vendors recommend: few-shot/multishot examples (3-5, in XML tags), XML-tagged content separation, placing long documents at the top of the prompt, and respecting message-role instruction hierarchies. Model-specific guidance matters: instruction-following models like GPT-4.1 reward literal, granular instructions (three agentic reminders added ~20% to OpenAI's internal SWE-bench), whereas reasoning models perform better with high-level goals than detailed direction. Emerging research also targets automated optimization of general system prompts (SPRIG), which is complementary to task-level prompt optimization.

## Findings
- **Prompt engineering steers LLMs via input text on the model's embedded knowledge, without parameter re-training or fine-tuning for a given task.** — confidence: high
  - Evidence: Vatsal & Dubey survey abstract states verbatim that prompt engineering 'does not require extensive parameter re-training or fine-tuning based on the given NLP task and thus solely operates on the embedded knowledge of LLMs.' Parameter-updating techniques (prompt tuning / soft prompts) are explicitly distinguished from prompt engineering, so they do not contradict the definitional claim. Corroborated by IBM and other sources noting prompt engineering operates 'at the interface layer instead of the parameter layer.'
  - Sources: https://arxiv.org/abs/2407.12994
- **The field has been systematically catalogued by multiple comprehensive surveys, which collectively organize dozens of distinct prompting techniques across many application domains and modalities.** — confidence: high
  - Evidence: Three primary survey sources, all unanimous (3-0). The Prompt Report (2406.06608) presents a PRISMA-based taxonomy of 58 text-only techniques plus 40 for other modalities. Sahoo et al. (2402.07927) categorize 41+ distinct techniques across exactly 12 application domains (reasoning/logic, hallucination reduction, code generation, etc.). Vatsal & Dubey (2407.12994) survey 44 research papers covering 39 prompting methods across 29 NLP tasks. Merged from original claims [3], [7], [11].
  - Sources: https://arxiv.org/html/2406.06608v1, https://arxiv.org/html/2402.07927v2, https://arxiv.org/abs/2407.12994
- **The Prompt Report empirically benchmarks prompting techniques against MMLU, providing experimental rather than purely descriptive evaluation.** — confidence: high
  - Evidence: Paper states 'we test a range of prompting techniques against the commonly used benchmark MMLU.' Six techniques (Zero-Shot, Zero-Shot-CoT, Few-Shot, Few-Shot-CoT, and Self-Consistency variants) were evaluated on a 2,800-question MMLU subset with gpt-3.5-turbo, producing concrete accuracy comparisons (Few-Shot-CoT highest; Zero-Shot-CoT underperformed plain Zero-Shot). Corroborated by emergentmind.com, learnprompting.org, alphaxiv.org.
  - Sources: https://arxiv.org/html/2406.06608v1
- **Chain-of-thought prompting (few-shot exemplars with intermediate reasoning steps) improves performance on arithmetic, commonsense, and symbolic reasoning — but is an emergent ability that only appears in sufficiently large models.** — confidence: high
  - Evidence: Primary source Wei et al. 2022 abstract: 'chain of thought prompting improves performance on a range of arithmetic, commonsense, and symbolic reasoning tasks' and 'reasoning abilities emerge naturally in sufficiently large language models.' Documented SOTA on GSM8K with a 540B model and eight CoT exemplars. The emergence claim was 2-1 (one dissent), but the dissent concerned distillation enabling small-model reasoning, which is a separate mechanism that qualifies rather than refutes the prompting-specific claim. CoT can hurt smaller models. Merged from claims [8] and [9]. Note: a related claim that PaLM-540B CoT reached 90.2% SOTA was refuted (0-3).
  - Sources: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2201.11903
- **Self-consistency substantially improves accuracy over baseline chain-of-thought: +17.9% on GSM8K, +11.0% on SVAMP, +12.2% on AQuA, and +6.4% on StrategyQA.** — confidence: high
  - Evidence: Survey 2402.07927 relays the figures verbatim; they trace to the primary source Wang et al. (ICLR 2023, arXiv 2203.11171), whose abstract reports the identical gains (also +3.9% ARC-challenge). Caveat: measured on 2022-era models (PaLM-540B, GPT-3), correctly attributed to that paper rather than presented as universal/current.
  - Sources: https://arxiv.org/html/2402.07927v2, https://arxiv.org/abs/2203.11171
- **Tree-of-Thoughts achieved a 74% success rate on the Game of 24 versus 4% for chain-of-thought, showing large gains from structured multi-path reasoning.** — confidence: high
  - Evidence: Original ToT paper (Yao et al., NeurIPS 2023, 2305.10601) abstract: 'in Game of 24, while GPT-4 with chain-of-thought prompting only solved 4% of tasks, our method achieved a success rate of 74%.' 74% is the best config (breadth b=5); the paper also reports 45% at b=1, but 74% is the authors' headline figure. Relayed accurately by survey 2402.07927.
  - Sources: https://arxiv.org/html/2402.07927v2, https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.10601
- **Few-shot / multishot prompting (3-5 well-crafted, relevant, diverse examples, wrapped in XML <example> tags) is an effective alternative to fine-tuning and dramatically improves output accuracy and consistency.** — confidence: high
  - Evidence: Two vendor primary sources, both unanimous. Anthropic: 'A few well-crafted examples ... can dramatically improve accuracy and consistency,' with qualifiers Relevant/Diverse/Structured (<example> tags) and 'Include 3-5 examples for best results.' OpenAI: 'Few-shot learning lets you steer a large language model toward a new task by including a handful of input/output examples in the prompt, rather than fine-tuning the model.' Caveat: peer-reviewed work (arXiv 2509.13196) qualifies that poorly chosen examples, ordering, or majority-label bias can degrade performance — reinforcing the 'well-crafted' condition rather than refuting it. The superlative 'dramatically' is unquantified vendor framing. Merged from claims [1] and [17].
  - Sources: https://platform.claude.com/docs/en/build-with-claude/prompt-engineering/claude-prompting-best-practices, https://developers.openai.com/api/docs/guides/prompt-engineering
- **Wrapping different content types in XML tags (e.g. <instructions>, <context>, <input>) helps the model parse complex prompts unambiguously and reduces misinterpretation.** — confidence: high
  - Evidence: Anthropic docs verbatim: 'XML tags help Claude parse complex prompts unambiguously, especially when your prompt mixes instructions, context, examples, and variable inputs. Wrapping each type of content in its own tag (e.g. <instructions>, <context>, <input>) reduces misinterpretation.' A calibrated mechanism-level claim (helps/reduces), not an absolute or quantified benchmark. Corroborated by Vellum, Walturn, AWS Bedrock blog.
  - Sources: https://platform.claude.com/docs/en/build-with-claude/prompt-engineering/claude-prompting-best-practices
- **Placing long documents/data near the top of a prompt (above the query, instructions, and examples) improves response quality, by up to ~30% in vendor tests for complex multi-document inputs.** — confidence: high
  - Evidence: Anthropic docs: 'Place your long documents and inputs near the top of your prompt ... This can significantly improve performance across all models,' with a Note: 'Queries at the end can improve response quality by up to 30% in tests, especially with complex, multi-document inputs.' Caveat: the 30% is an uncited internal benchmark with no published methodology; the claim faithfully attributes it as 'in tests.' Scoped to large/data-rich inputs (20k+ tokens).
  - Sources: https://platform.claude.com/docs/en/build-with-claude/prompt-engineering/claude-prompting-best-practices
- **Prompts have a message-role instruction hierarchy: developer messages are prioritized ahead of user messages, establishing an instruction priority order.** — confidence: high
  - Evidence: OpenAI prompt-engineering guide verbatim: 'developer messages are instructions provided by the application developer, prioritized ahead of user messages,' establishing developer > user > assistant. Corroborated by OpenAI's Model Spec (2025-02-12 through 2025-12-18: 'developer messages have greater authority,' Platform > Developer > User) and the Instruction Hierarchy research (arXiv 2404.13208). Minor nuance: the full hierarchy includes platform/root levels above developer.
  - Sources: https://developers.openai.com/api/docs/guides/prompt-engineering, https://model-spec.openai.com, https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.13208
- **Model class changes how prompts should be written: reasoning models perform well with high-level goals and less granular instruction, whereas GPT/instruction-following models need precise, granular guidance — and GPT-4.1 in particular follows instructions more literally than predecessors.** — confidence: high
  - Evidence: OpenAI docs: 'A reasoning model is like a senior co-worker ... A GPT model is like a junior coworker. They'll perform best with explicit instructions,' and 'reasoning models generally excel with less granular direction compared to GPT variants.' GPT-4.1 guide: 'GPT-4.1 is trained to follow instructions more closely and more literally than its predecessors.' Academic support: arXiv 2512.02246 finds detailed prompts slightly hurt reasoning-model performance. Both vendor primary sources; the GPT-4.1 training characterization is OpenAI's self-description. Merged from claims [18] and [20].
  - Sources: https://developers.openai.com/api/docs/guides/prompt-engineering, https://cookbook.openai.com/examples/gpt4-1_prompting_guide
- **Adding three agentic system-prompt reminders (persistence, tool-calling, planning) increased OpenAI's internal SWE-bench Verified score by close to 20% on GPT-4.1.** — confidence: high
  - Evidence: GPT-4.1 Prompting Guide verbatim: 'these three simple instructions ... increased our internal SWE-bench Verified score by close to 20%.' Vote was 2-1; the dissent reflects that this is a self-reported internal benchmark not independently reproduced, but the claim is honestly scoped as 'OpenAI's internal' and 'close to 20%.' Treat the magnitude as vendor-reported, not independently verified.
  - Sources: https://cookbook.openai.com/examples/gpt4-1_prompting_guide
- **Rephrase and Respond (RaR) — letting an LLM rephrase/expand a human question then answer it in a single prompt — significantly improves response quality across multiple models and a wide range of tasks.** — confidence: high
  - Evidence: Primary source (Zhao et al., UCLA, Nov 2023) abstract: RaR 'allows LLMs to rephrase and expand questions posed by humans and provide responses in a single prompt' and 'methods significantly improve the performance of different models across a wide range to tasks.' Tested on GPT-4-0613, GPT-3.5-turbo, Vicuna-13b across ~10 tasks. Caveat: gains are uneven (GPT-4 benefits most), weaker models can alter question intent, and simple questions can be over-complicated — qualifications, not refutations. Merged from claims [12] and [13].
  - Sources: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2311.04205, https://uclaml.github.io/Rephrase-and-Respond
- **SPRIG is an edit-based genetic algorithm that automatically optimizes general system prompts from prespecified components, and system-prompt optimization is complementary to task-level prompt optimization (combining them yields further gains).** — confidence: high
  - Evidence: Primary source (Zhang et al., ICLR 2026) abstract: 'SPRIG, an edit-based genetic algorithm that iteratively constructs prompts from prespecified components to maximize the model's performance in general scenarios' and 'combining system and task-level optimizations leads to further improvement, which showcases their complementary nature.' Evaluated across 42 benchmarks / 7 categories on Llama3.1-8B, Mistral-Nemo, Qwen2.5-7B; ~28% of questions answered by only one strategy evidences complementarity. Caveat: benefits weaken at 70B/72B scale; arXiv preprint. Note: a stronger claim that SPRIG can substitute for per-task prompt engineering was refuted (0-3). Merged from claims [14] and [15].
  - Sources: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2410.14826
- **Anthropic recommends three prerequisites before prompt engineering — clear success criteria, ways to empirically test against them, and a first-draft prompt — and documents a core technique set (clarity, examples, XML structuring, role prompting, thinking, prompt chaining).** — confidence: high
  - Evidence: Anthropic overview docs verbatim: 'This guide assumes that you have: 1. A clear definition of the success criteria ... 2. Some ways to empirically test against those criteria 3. A first draft prompt you want to improve. If not, we highly suggest you spend time establishing that first.' And: 'All prompting techniques — from clarity and examples to XML structuring, role prompting, thinking, and prompt chaining — are covered in Prompting best practices.' Minor framing nuance: docs say 'assumes' but follow with 'highly suggest.' Merged from claims [21] and [22].
  - Sources: https://docs.anthropic.com/en/docs/build-with-claude/prompt-engineering/overview

## Caveats
Most quantified benchmark figures are model- and time-specific: self-consistency and CoT numbers were measured on 2022-era models (PaLM-540B, GPT-3); the ToT 74% is the best-configuration headline number (45% at the lowest breadth); and several percentages are accuracy gains on specific benchmark subsets, not universal results. Two improvement figures are vendor-reported and not independently reproduced: Anthropic's 'up to 30%' long-context gain and OpenAI's 'close to 20%' SWE-bench gain are uncited internal benchmarks with no published methodology — treat both as directional, not verified magnitudes (the SWE-bench claim also drew a dissenting vote, 2-1). Vendor-documentation claims (Anthropic, OpenAI) are authoritative for those companies' own models but are partly self-description of training and capabilities, not third-party verification, and the 'dramatically improves' superlative for few-shot is unquantified marketing-adjacent framing. The CoT-emergence claim was 2-1, and survey counts vary slightly from their abstracts (e.g. 'over 41' techniques vs. exactly 41). SPRIG is an arXiv preprint (ICLR 2026 listed). Two related claims were refuted by 0-3 votes and excluded: a PaLM-540B 90.2% SOTA figure, and the assertion that SPRIG general optimization can substitute for per-task prompt engineering. Few-shot efficacy is conditional — poorly chosen/ordered examples can degrade performance.

## Open questions
- What is the published methodology behind the unverified vendor figures (Anthropic's 'up to 30%' long-context gain and OpenAI's 'close to 20%' SWE-bench gain) — which models, datasets, and baselines were used?
- How well do the headline benchmark gains for self-consistency, CoT, and ToT (measured largely on 2022-2023 models like PaLM-540B, GPT-3.5, GPT-4) transfer to current frontier reasoning models that already perform internal reasoning?
- Under what specific conditions does few-shot prompting hurt rather than help (example ordering, majority-label bias, over-prompting reasoning models), and are there robust rules for when to omit examples?
- Does automated system-prompt optimization like SPRIG hold up at larger model scales (the paper notes weakening benefits at 70B/72B) and in independent, peer-reviewed replications?
